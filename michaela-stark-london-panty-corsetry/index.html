The Season / London

Michaela Stark, and the body as its own couture

By Isabelle Rowe 21 April 2026 Fashion
← Back to The Edit

A studio in London. A corset that insists the body is already finished. Michaela Stark has spent the better part of a decade accentuating the parts of a woman that fashion keeps sanding away, and Issue 76 of 10 Magazine makes the case that the change she is making is the one the industry cannot quite keep pace with.

Stark grew up in Australia and crossed the world to build a couture practice in London, which turns out to be where her sensibility was always going to land. The British capital has, for fifty years, kept space for designers who treat the body as a problem of sculpture. Stark slots into that lineage and then bends it outward.

Her work looks, at first glance, like a corset practice. It reads, at second glance, like an essay about whose bodies get invited into the room. The stays, bones and lacings she uses are historical quotations. Stark flips the direction of the argument. The lumps, as she puts it, are the point.

Hackney, and a couturier's eye

Stark's London is not the West End. It is an east-side city laced together by Victoria Park, Bethnal Green and a stretch of De Beauvoir Town where a seamstress with a long memory can still find a trim wholesaler who knows her name. She trained as a seamstress first, and it shows. Under the provocation there is a craft that is older than the photographs suggest, and that craft is the reason the garments hold together in the hand after they have already spoken on the page.

She has described Leigh Bowery's Tate Modern retrospective as the catalyst that reminded her what performance is for. Perfectionism sometimes really kills the joy in the work, she told Dazed. The line is worth keeping, because it explains what her Instagram followers see and what her ateliers sew. The work is built for motion, and the finish of the clothes reflects that intent.

Panty, the lingerie label that does the maths

Alongside the couture commissions, Stark runs a lingerie label called Panty. The name is casual, though the operation is anything but. Panty runs sizes XXS to 5XL with in-house grading, which is to say that Stark has hired the technical team the rest of the industry claims it cannot find. The pieces are cut, fitted and graded across the full range before they go to production, rather than scaled up afterwards. It is the detail every size-inclusive brand promises, and almost none deliver on.

Panty sells lace knickers at lingerie prices and bodysuits that register as sculpture once a body fills them. The pricing is deliberately accessible for a designer who charges couture rates on the other side of the studio. Stark has said plainly that the standard sample size 2 is killing the industry's creativity, and the Panty range is where she is proving it, one order at a time.

Spread across London

In January she released a 2026 calendar called Spread Across London. Twelve photographs, shot with cinematographer Nora Nord and styled with Jessica Temple, place Stark herself, often partially unclothed, at public sites across the city: the South Bank outside Saint Paul's; Parliament House in Westminster; Victoria Park on a grey morning; a square in Clapton; the Bank district at dusk. The pictures are funny before they are provocative. They are also a quiet piece of political writing.

The subtext is simple. Instagram keeps removing her images. Street harassment in London, she has said, is worsening. The calendar argues that a body posed in the middle of the city, on its own terms, is still an act of reclamation. It would read as naive if Stark did not sew the cloth herself. She does. The argument shows up in the literal stitching on her own body, and the picture earns its point.

The corset, reconsidered

The move Stark has pulled off, collection by collection, is to detach the corset from its reputation. In her hands the stays are rendered as sculpture. They emphasise the belly, the hips, the places Victorian pattern-making tried to erase. The garments are structured, and the structure is celebratory.

“

We're done with this old one-size standard of beauty, as it's killing the creativity of fashion.

Michaela Stark, 10 Magazine Issue 76

This is what the Tate and the Fondazione Sozzani have begun to recognise. Stark has shown at both, and a museum context gives the work its correct frame. The clothes belong in an exhibition because they carry a question about bodies that one cannot easily settle while wearing them. Put them on, and the question follows you around the room.

The Change Makers, 10 Magazine Issue 76. Photography by Michaela Stark / 10 Magazine, Issue 76

The long read

The moment Stark is meeting is a particular one. The industry has spent three seasons insisting it has fixed its relationship with bodies and three seasons casting the same silhouettes regardless. The clothes she sends into the room are a question, stitched in boning and mesh, delivered from a studio in Hackney to anyone willing to look for long enough to see it.

She does not need the industry to accept the proposition. The industry, quietly, is beginning to.
