Vatísimo — Willy Chavarria brings Chicano pride to the high street

By Elena Voss 28 March 2026 Fashion
← Back to The Edit

When Zara announces a designer collaboration, the fashion world tends to lean in with a mixture of curiosity and mild suspicion — wondering whether the result will be genuine or merely decorative. With Willy Chavarria and the collection he has named Vatísimo, the answer is something considerably more interesting than either.

The collection landed in stores on 26 March 2026, accompanied by a campaign shot by Glen Luchford and featuring Christy Turlington and Alberto Guerra in a visual world drawn from the heightened emotional register of 1980s American prime-time drama and the telenovelas Chavarria watched as a child in Fresno, California. It is a specific and personal set of references, and that specificity is the point. This is not a collaboration built on the neutral vocabulary of contemporary luxury — the beige, the minimalism, the studied absence of cultural content. It is built on a particular story, told from a particular place.

The word behind the name

Vatísimo is the superlative of vato — a term rooted in Chicano vernacular that speaks to kinship, community, and the kind of loyalty that is chosen rather than inherited. It is a word that belongs to a specific cultural register, one that has historically been overlooked by the fashion industry even as that industry helped itself liberally to the aesthetic codes it produced. Chavarria, who is the son of a Mexican immigrant father and an Irish mother, is not borrowing from that register. He grew up inside it. The distinction matters.

The title announces the collection’s ambition before a single garment is seen. This is not a crossover exercise in which an independent designer lends a house his visual vocabulary for a season. It is an attempt to move a set of values — about dignity, about belonging, about the relationship between cultural identity and how we dress — into the broadest possible distribution channel. Zara operates at a scale that independent labels, however critically lauded, simply cannot reach. Chavarria appears to have understood that scale as an opportunity rather than a compromise.

The collection itself

The garments bear his unmistakable hand. The silhouette favours the kind of broad-shouldered tailoring that references American workwear of the 1970s and 1980s — structured blazers with padded shoulders cut to a deliberately macho proportion, wide-leg trousers in heavy wool blends, and three-quarter length chinos that carry the ease of leisure without sacrificing their formal spine. Against these anchoring pieces, Chavarria introduces womenswear that moves with greater fluidity: a lace camisole dress in salmon pink, a silk paisley bandana, feminine slips in materials that catch light differently from every angle.

The fabrication is more considered than one might expect at this price point. The ready-to-wear line draws on Italian fabrics alongside cupro — a semi-synthetic with a silky hand and better environmental credentials than conventional viscose — and premium leather for outerwear and accessories. Short-sleeved shirts arrive with raw-edge finishing, a detail that reads as studied undress: the deliberate unpolish of someone who knows exactly what polish looks like. Structured pencil skirts in russet leather and leather clogs complete a wardrobe that manages to feel cohesive across its tonal range, from the graphic boldness of the branded tees and hoodies to the quieter confidence of the tailored separates.

The campaign and its world

The visual language Luchford and Chavarria have constructed for the campaign deserves its own consideration. The 1980s dramas that inspired it — Dallas, Dynasty, and their telenovela equivalents — were not, by the aesthetic standards of contemporary fashion photography, subtle. They were all poolside confrontation, sharp eyewear, dresses that announced their wearer’s intentions from across a marble lobby. What Chavarria finds in that tradition is not camp nostalgia but something more structural: a conviction that clothes should have feelings, that drama is not a failure of taste but an expression of emotional intelligence.

Turlington, who has spent four decades as a cipher for a certain kind of cool, appears here in a different register entirely — not removed or untouchable, but present, warm, implicated in a story that is unfolding around her. Guerra brings a masculine energy that complements rather than dominates. The result is a campaign that feels genuinely cinematic in its attention to the relationships between people, rather than simply using people as surfaces on which to display clothing.

What this collaboration means

It is worth noting what Zara is becoming under its current collaborative strategy. The pairing with John Galliano — however controversial in its cultural implications — positioned the retailer as a platform for genuine fashion ideas rather than their diluted approximation. The Stefano Pilati collaboration offered a masterclass in the difference between minimalism as discipline and minimalism as the absence of thought. With Chavarria, Zara is doing something it has not quite done before: giving significant reach to a designer whose work is explicitly political, whose subject is the relationship between identity and visibility, and whose aesthetic cannot be separated from the community that produced it.

Prices run from below fifty dollars for the graphic tees to two hundred and fifty for the more considered leather pieces — a range that makes the collection genuinely accessible rather than accessible in name only. A dedicated pop-up at 73 Spring Street in SoHo has been supplemented by a global retail rollout, which means that Chavarria’s argument — about who fashion belongs to and what it is capable of saying — is now available in most major cities on the planet. That is not nothing. That is, in fact, quite a lot.

Fashion’s relationship with the cultures it draws from has rarely been an equitable one. Chicano aesthetics have influenced American style for decades, appearing in everything from workwear to luxury menswear to the vocabulary of contemporary streetwear, with attribution that has been, at best, inconsistent. Vatísimo does not correct that history, but it complicates the usual transaction. Here is a designer writing his own story, in his own language, at a scale that demands to be taken seriously. The least the industry can do is pay attention.
