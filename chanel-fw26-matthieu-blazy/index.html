Chanel FW26 — Matthieu Blazy and the Art of Becoming

By Isabelle Rowe 29 March 2026 8 min read
← Back to The Edit

At the Grand Palais on the ninth of March, under a vaulted glass ceiling strung with primary-coloured cranes, Matthieu Blazy delivered a second Chanel collection that did something far more interesting than impress: it convinced. The argument he is making — that Chanel is not a monument to be preserved but a living contradiction to be inhabited — has found its footing, and the fashion world is still catching its breath.

There is a particular kind of pressure that descends on a designer at their second show. The debut, however successful, carries the latitude of the newcomer. The sophomore collection is where the thesis is either consolidated or revealed as a single good idea. Matthieu Blazy, who arrived at the rue Cambon last year as only the fourth creative director in Chanel's 116-year history, is demonstrating with some confidence that his idea is not singular but structural — a way of reading the house that has enough depth to sustain seasons of work.

He opened with an image that was almost insultingly simple: a black jersey dress, unadorned, cut with the kind of precision that announces its intelligence only to those already attuned to it. From there, the collection unfolded as a sustained conversation about what Chanel means when it contradicts itself — and it contradicts itself constantly, richly, and on purpose.

The caterpillar and the butterfly

Blazy's show notes quoted Gabrielle Chanel from an interview she gave to Le Figaro in the 1950s: "Fashion is both caterpillar and butterfly. We need dresses that crawl and dresses that fly." It is the kind of remark that, in less capable hands, becomes a mood board caption. Here it was a structural logic, applied across eighty-four looks with the consistency of an argument rather than the looseness of a theme.

The caterpillar — the Chanel of function, of daily life, of the woman who invented jersey as a fabric for freedom rather than leisure — arrived in the form of masculine-coded work shirts cut from the house's most iconic bouclé tweed, of blouson jackets and low-slung skirts that borrowed their ease from the garçonne silhouette of a century ago. There was a directness to these pieces that felt almost confrontational in a season when luxury fashion has tended toward complication.

Then came the butterflies: dresses in iridescent metal mesh that caught the Grand Palais light and broke it into colour; coats threaded with lurex and silicone that moved with a quality somewhere between tailoring and sculpture; a closing gown in black silk with an open back and a single camellia that managed to be both the most Chanel thing in the room and the most unmistakably Blazy.

A construction site reimagined

The set deserves its own consideration. The Grand Palais's permanent grandeur is one of fashion's most difficult backdrops — it has a way of overwhelming shows that don't meet it on its own terms. Blazy's solution was to introduce the primary-coloured cranes of an active construction site, their lacquered surfaces gleaming against the nineteenth-century ironwork above. The floor was opalescent, rippling with a quality of light that evoked Monet's water studies without committing to the reference.

The effect was not dissonance but dialogue. The cranes spoke of transformation in progress, of a house being not torn down but built upon — the same argument, translated into scenography. It is a rare thing in fashion to find a set that does intellectual work rather than merely atmospheric work, and rarer still for that work to advance rather than simply illustrate the designer's thesis.

Lady Gaga's "Just Dance" arrived on the soundtrack at a moment calculated to produce a particular kind of joy, which it did. The front row — Jennie of BLACKPINK occupying a position that confirmed Chanel's commercial instincts remain as sharp as its aesthetic ones — responded with the kind of energy that fashion weeks occasionally and unpredictably generate, and that the internet then sustains for days afterward.

The accessories argument

Accessories at Chanel carry a weight that accessories at almost no other house do, because the bags are not merely commercial vehicles but cultural objects — the 2.55, the 11.12, the Boy, each with its own history and its own devoted constituency. Blazy handled this inheritance with a confidence that suggested he has been thinking about it for some time.

The flap bags returned in their pristine, original condition, pointedly departing from the deliberately distressed versions he sent down the runway last season. The message was clear enough: that subversion, once registered, does not need to be repeated. A hybrid bag that fused the hardware of Gabrielle Chanel's original 1955 design with the silhouette of Karl Lagerfeld's 1983 iteration was the season's most quietly audacious move — a piece that asked whether the house's two defining eras might coexist rather than compete. The answer, in quilted black leather, appeared to be yes.

The shoes — metallic cap-toed heels, sock-like two-tone boots, the V-vamp pump updated in mule and ankle-boot iterations — were the season's most commercially obvious hits, the kind that generate wait-list conversations within hours of the show's close. They were also, it should be noted, very good.

What the second season tells us

The critical reception that greeted Blazy's debut last October was warm but contained an audible note of reservation: the work was impressive, but could he sustain it? Could a designer whose sensibility was formed entirely outside the rue Cambon make peace with its peculiarities without either surrendering to them or conducting a renovation that its custodians would not sanction?

Fall 2026 answers those questions with a definiteness that the debut, necessarily provisional, could not achieve. Blazy is not dismantling Chanel. He is not conducting a hostile takeover of the house's identity in the name of his own. He is doing something more interesting and more difficult: he is showing that its contradictions — between rigour and ease, between masculine codes and feminine expression, between heritage and invention — were always its greatest strength, and that no one had simply trusted them enough to make the contradiction itself the point.

Gabrielle Chanel, who built an empire on borrowed vocabulary and revolutionary instinct, would likely have found the argument persuasive. The collection, which by all accounts is selling with the urgency that follows a show that the industry has collectively decided matters, suggests that the customer has arrived at the same conclusion.

The edit

The standout pieces are the ones that occupy Blazy's conceptual middle ground: neither pure caterpillar nor fully unfurled butterfly, but something suspended in the moment of transition. The bouclé blouson worn over a drop-waist silk skirt. The ribbed knit coat with lurex threading that catches light differently depending on the angle. The jersey dress that opened the show — which will, in all probability, become one of those pieces that designers spend subsequent seasons trying to forget was not their idea first.

The collection's closing image — that black silk dress, the camellia, the open back — is the kind of thing that ends up in retrospectives. Whether Blazy's tenure at Chanel becomes the subject of a retrospective is a question that will take years to answer. On the basis of what arrived at the Grand Palais on the ninth of March, the answer is beginning to look very much like yes.

Chanel Fall/Winter 2026 was presented at the Grand Palais, Paris, on 9 March 2026. The collection is available through Chanel boutiques worldwide and at chanel.com.
