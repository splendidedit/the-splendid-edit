Creative Director — Farewell

The Di Felice Years: how Courrèges came back to life

By Léa Fontaine 27 March 2026 Fashion
← Back to The Edit

Nicolas Di Felice has stepped down as artistic director of Courrèges after five years — and his final collection, a cinematic study of a Parisian woman’s day from the first pale light to the last Metro home, arrived as something between a manifesto and a love letter. It was, by any measure, the most assured farewell the house could have asked for. The question now is not what he leaves behind, but whether anyone can match what he built.

There is a particular cruelty to the timing of a great designer’s departure. It rarely arrives at a low point — at a moment when the work has grown stale and the house has drifted beyond recovery. More often, it comes precisely when the vocabulary is sharpest, when the collections have found their register and the audience has learned how to read them. Di Felice’s exit from Courrèges follows that second, more difficult pattern. He leaves not because the project has exhausted itself, but because five years at the same frequency is, perhaps, enough.

When he arrived in 2021, Courrèges was a house with more history than momentum. André Courrèges had invented something radical in the 1960s — geometric silhouettes, white vinyl, a vision of the future that felt simultaneously utopian and precise — but the label had spent decades since unable to locate the contemporary relevance hidden inside that inheritance. The Space Age belonged to another era, and every attempt to translate it risked either pastiche or irrelevance. Di Felice, a Belgian designer who had trained under Raf Simons and spent years at Louis Vuitton before arriving in Paris, understood that the problem was never the archive. It was the courage to use it differently.

The collection as coda

His final offering — titled 24 Hours in the Life of a Courrèges Woman — was structured like a film. Guests arrived to find a clock in place of the usual invitation card. The set replicated a Paris street: narrow, functional, scored with manholes and pavement markings. The soundtrack moved through the textures of an ordinary day, coffee percolating into silence, the half-heard cadence of a radio news bulletin, the particular quality of late-afternoon light. Di Felice was channelling the Belgian filmmaker Chantal Akerman, whose work has always been preoccupied with the drama inside the quotidian, the extraordinary pressure contained in the seemingly unremarkable hours.

The clothes matched that ambition. Slim coats with funnel collars arrived in glossy vinyls, their proportions as precise as a ruled line. Pleated skirts moved with a slick mechanical rhythm. Evening dresses were assembled from organza printed with Paris Metro tickets and cloakroom stubs — fragments of the city, elevated into something both witty and genuinely beautiful. A new bag, the Shadow, appeared to mold to its contents, as though the object were defined not by its own shape but by what it held.

For the finale, every look reappeared in white — a choice that Di Felice framed as an open canvas, a gesture toward whatever identity the house might assume next. It was a generous exit: a designer who has decided to leave without pulling the building down around him, offering the space something clean to inherit.

Five years, one language

What Di Felice constructed over his tenure was not a revival in the conventional sense. He did not attempt to reconstruct the original Courrèges with archaeological precision, nor did he abandon the house’s identity in pursuit of a broader commercial appeal. Instead, he found a way to make the Space Age idiom speak to the present without translating it into nostalgia. The vinyl stayed — but it arrived in cuts that felt contemporary rather than retro. The geometric rigour remained — but it was offset by a clubby sensibility that was entirely his own, a willingness to let the clothes exist at night as comfortably as they did by day.

The pieces that defined his tenure were not simply good garments. They were proposals: the vinyl jacket that became the label’s most recognisable object under his watch, the bags that accumulated a devoted following among women who had never previously thought of Courrèges as a house they needed, the runway presentations that consistently rewarded close attention without demanding it. He made Courrèges relevant to a generation that has no particular investment in its history — which is the hardest and most necessary thing a creative director can do.

What the house inherits

Courrèges is owned by the Artemis group, the Pinault family’s holding company, and there is every indication that the house intends to continue with the seriousness of purpose that has characterised its recent years. A successor, the house has indicated, will be announced imminently. The list of names in circulation is long, as it always is at moments like this; the list of designers who could actually continue the project that Di Felice began is considerably shorter.

What any incoming creative director inherits is not simply a set of archives and a signature material. They inherit an audience that has been educated in a particular language, a set of expectations about what Courrèges should feel like and what problems it should be trying to solve. That is simultaneously a gift and a constraint — the best kind of brief a designer can receive.

The next chapter

Di Felice himself departs with considerable credit in the industry’s informal ledger. He is set to appear as guest designer at Jean Paul Gaultier’s next haute couture presentation, an invitation that carries a particular weight — Gaultier’s guest programme has become one of the few genuinely prestigious platforms for a designer between commitments, a chance to work without the institutional pressures that shape everything at a major house. Speculation about a more permanent appointment has already begun to circulate, with both Alaïa and Rabanne mentioned in conversations that may or may not be grounded in anything more than wishful thinking.

Whatever comes next, Di Felice has demonstrated something important: that it is possible to spend five years at a heritage house without being consumed by it. He arrived with a point of view, maintained it against the considerable institutional pressure to dilute it, and leaves with that point of view intact. The Space Age, in his hands, never became a costume. It became a living argument. That is the only kind of legacy worth having.

The white finale was not an ending. It was an invitation — to whatever designer, whatever vision, whatever version of Courrèges comes next. The blank canvas is one of the most generous things a departing artist can offer. It requires only that the person who accepts it know what colour they intend to bring.
