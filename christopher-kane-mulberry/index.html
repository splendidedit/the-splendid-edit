Christopher Kane at Mulberry — the appointment that makes sense of everything

By Margaux Delacroix 27 March 2026 Fashion
← Back to The Edit

Mulberry has named Christopher Kane creative director of womenswear. The Scottish designer — whose own label closed in 2023 after nearly two decades of operation — arrives at Somerset’s most famous leather house with a first collection due in September and a point to prove about what British fashion looks like when it stops trying to be anything else.

There is a particular kind of fashion appointment that provokes immediate consensus. A designer whose talent has never been in question takes a role at a house whose potential has never been in doubt, and the reaction is not surprise but something closer to relief. The Kane-Mulberry pairing belongs to that category. It is the kind of decision that, once announced, makes you wonder why it took so long.

Kane graduated from Central Saint Martins in 2006 with a collection that the industry still references — neon-bandage dresses that managed to be both confrontational and deeply considered. Within three years he was designing Versace’s Versus line. By 2013 Kering had acquired a majority stake in his business, a move that positioned him alongside the conglomerate’s stable of established names. He was thirty-one years old.

What followed was more complicated. Kane bought back his label in 2018, weathered the pandemic’s impact on independent fashion, closed his Mount Street flagship in 2020, and ultimately shuttered the brand entirely three years later. It was a trajectory that could be read as failure only by those who misunderstand how precarious the economics of independent design have become. The clothes were never the problem. The model was.

Why Mulberry needs this

Mulberry has spent the better part of a decade searching for the right creative temperature. The house — founded in 1971, rooted in English craft, built on the back of bags that became cultural shorthand for a certain kind of understated luxury — has at times seemed uncertain about whether it wants to be a heritage brand or a fashion one. The answer, of course, is that the distinction is false. The most compelling houses in the world are both.

Under CEO Andrea Baldo, Mulberry has been quietly restructuring its operations with a discipline that the wider industry has largely overlooked. The Somerset factory, where much of the brand’s leather goods are still produced, remains one of the few luxury manufacturing facilities of its scale operating on British soil. The supply chain is shorter, the provenance is genuine, and the story writes itself — provided there is a designer at the centre of it who can make the product feel urgent rather than merely dependable.

Kane is that designer. His sensibility — which has always operated at the intersection of the cerebral and the visceral, the sculptural and the wearable — is precisely what Mulberry needs to move from respected to desired. He has spent a career making clothes that reward a second look, that contain ideas you discover only after you’ve worn them for a week. That quality, applied to a house with Mulberry’s manufacturing depth, could produce something genuinely distinctive.

The September question

Kane’s first collection is due in September, with product arriving in stores by January 2027. That timeline is brisk but not reckless — it suggests a designer who has been thinking about this kind of role for longer than the announcement implies, and a house that has been preparing the infrastructure to support it.

The immediate question is what Kane does with accessories. Mulberry’s identity has been built on bags — the Bayswater, the Alexa, the various iterations that have defined the brand in the popular imagination. Kane’s appointment is specifically for womenswear, but any creative director who inherits a house with that kind of accessory heritage will inevitably reshape how those objects are presented, styled, and understood. The relationship between the clothes and the bags will tell us a great deal about Kane’s ambition.

What this says about London

There is a broader story here about the state of London fashion. The city has spent years watching its most talented designers leave for Paris or Milan, absorbed into conglomerates that offer the financial stability London cannot. Kane’s appointment at Mulberry reverses that current. It places one of the most inventive designers of his generation inside a British house, working with British manufacturing, for a British audience that has been quietly waiting for this kind of alignment.

It also sends a signal about what happens after the independent label. The old narrative — that a designer who loses their own house has somehow failed — is being replaced by something more honest and more interesting: the recognition that the most compelling creative work often happens within institutions rather than despite them. Kane’s time at his own label gave him fluency. Mulberry gives him resources, scale, and a heritage worth interpreting.

His debut in September will be watched closely and judged generously. The industry wants this to work, not out of sentimentality but because the alternative — another British house drifting without creative direction while another gifted designer sits idle — serves nobody. The pieces are in place. The Somerset kilns are warm. The rest, as they say, is tailoring.
