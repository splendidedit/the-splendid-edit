The Stay — Los Angeles

Public West Hollywood — luxury for all on the Sunset Strip

By Camille Ashworth 27 March 2026 Travel
← Back to The Edit

Ian Schrager has spent forty years defining what a hotel should feel like. With Public West Hollywood — his second property under the Public brand, designed in collaboration with John Pawson and opening this spring on the Sunset Strip — he appears to be asking a different question entirely: what a hotel should cost, and for whom the best design in the world should be made available.

The premise is deceptively simple. Take the aesthetic standards of a two-thousand-dollar-a-night hotel — the proportions, the materials, the quality of light in a lobby at six in the evening — and deliver them at a price that does not require a financial reckoning before booking. Schrager has described it as democratising luxury. What it actually represents is something more radical: the argument that exclusivity was never the point of good design. It was merely a side effect of laziness.

Public West Hollywood occupies a landmark building on Sunset Boulevard, the stretch of road that has accumulated more mythology per linear metre than perhaps any other in America. The 137-room property sits at the junction of the Strip’s legacy — the Chateau Marmont, the Viper Room, the Mondrian — and its increasingly uncertain future. Where older hotels in the neighbourhood have tended to trade on nostalgia, Schrager appears to be trading on the opposite: the conviction that the next interesting thing to happen on Sunset Boulevard has not happened yet.

The Pawson principle

John Pawson’s involvement is not incidental. The British architect, whose work has always been concerned with the relationship between space and stillness, has collaborated with Schrager on the Edition hotels — properties that operate at the top of the market and charge accordingly. Public asks him to apply the same rigour at a fundamentally different price point, and the result is instructive about what minimalism actually means when the budget changes.

John Pawson — interior design for the West Hollywood property — Photography courtesy of Wallpaper*

The answer, it turns out, is that it doesn’t. Pawson’s approach has never depended on expensive finishes; it depends on proportion, on the discipline of knowing what to leave out, on the quality of a shadow falling across a wall at a particular hour. Those are free. What costs money in most luxury hotels is ornamentation, and Pawson has built a career on its absence. The all-white lobby is a case in point: it achieves its effect through volume and light rather than through materials that announce their own expense. Guest rooms introduce saturated colour through furniture and textiles — warm ochres, deep terracotta, the kind of blues that Los Angeles produces at dusk — against backgrounds that are deliberately restrained.

What Schrager knows

Schrager’s career is the most interesting throughline in modern hospitality. Studio 54 taught him that a room is only as good as the energy of the people in it. The Morgans Hotel Group, which he co-founded in the 1980s, invented the boutique hotel as a category. The Edition, developed with Marriott, proved that a designer hotel could operate at corporate scale without losing its nerve. Each chapter has been a refinement of a single idea: that the experience of being somewhere should be designed with as much intention as the building itself.

Public is the logical conclusion of that trajectory. If the boutique hotel was about making luxury personal, Public is about making personal luxury universal. The 16,000-square-foot open-air rooftop terrace — with panoramic views across Los Angeles, from the Hollywood sign to the Pacific — is not reserved for suite guests or members of a private club. It is there for everyone who walks through the door. Three restaurants and bars operate on the same principle: the cocktail list does not vary by room category.

Public Hotel — communal spaces where the social energy is the amenity — Photography courtesy of Wallpaper*

The Los Angeles question

Los Angeles is a city that has always understood the relationship between design and access better than New York gives it credit for. The Case Study houses, the midcentury modern movement, the entire tradition of California architecture has been rooted in the idea that light, space, and proportion belong to everyone — not just those who can afford a Richard Neutra commission. Public West Hollywood enters that conversation with a hospitality proposition that feels native to the city in a way that many imported hotel brands do not.

The timing matters too. West Hollywood is in the middle of a generational shift. The old guard — the hotels that survived on rock-and-roll mythology and rooftop pool parties — are being joined by a new cohort of properties that take their food seriously, their design seriously, and their neighbourhoods seriously. Public does not arrive as a disruptor. It arrives as a clarification: this is what Sunset Boulevard looks like when someone pays attention to it for the right reasons.

Whether the model sustains itself will depend, as always, on the Tuesday morning test — the ordinary stay, the midweek business traveller, the repeat guest who returns not because of the opening-night energy but because the shower pressure is right and the coffee arrives at the temperature they expect. Schrager has passed that test before, at every price point the industry offers. There is no particular reason to believe he won’t pass it here. The Sunset Strip, for once, may have found a hotel that is more interested in its future than its past.
